Bassleer Rotation LogicRegular is the backbone — 3× per week. Chlorella 3× for algae-based micronutrients and omega-3s. Acai 3× for colour enhancement and antioxidants. Garlic capped at 2× per week — therapeutic/immune food, not a daily staple.
Black Worms — Wed & Sat2× per week is the sweet spot at 59% protein — more causes over-conditioning. The ram and gourami will go crazy for these. Drop mid-column and they sink slowly giving all zones access.
BBS — Thu & SatDrop the frozen cube slightly away from the main feeding area. At nauplius stage they suspend in the water column — this is where the blue eyes shine, directly mimicking their natural insect prey. 2× per week without wasting your hatch supply.
AquaGel Super Greens — Tue, Fri, SunDrop one frozen cube near the bottom. The ottos, bristlenose and cories will graze on it as it softens. The 38% spirulina makes this the primary algae/plant nutrition for the whole tank. Ottos need consistent plant matter — 3× per week ensures they're never short.
Algae Crisps — Mon, Wed, Fri, SunBottom-level anchor 4× per week. Place near the bristlenose territory. Cories and loaches will also graze. On AquaGel days, reduce to ⅓ to avoid over-feeding the bottom zone.
Cull Shrimp — no dedicated feed neededThey scavenge everything that reaches the bottom. Vitalis catfish pellets, algae crisps and gel cubes are all shrimp-safe and will sustain them without dedicated shrimp food.

🦐 Brine Shrimp Hatchery

1 litre · Perth tap water · WA lake salt

💧

Water Recipe

Perth tap water · WA lake salt · 1 litre
1
Dechlorinate tap water
Fill your hatchery with 1L of Perth tap water. Add a single drop of water conditioner (e.g. Seachem Prime). Perth tap is chloraminated — just leaving it out overnight is not reliable enough, conditioner is the safe choice.
2
Add WA lake salt
Measure out 1½ tablespoons (≈ 25g) to 2 tablespoons (≈ 30g) of your WA lake salt (99% NaCl). This gives 25–30 ppt salinity — the ideal range for Artemia hatching. At 99% NaCl it's actually cleaner than most aquarium salts. Stir or swirl until fully dissolved before adding eggs.
3
Check & adjust pH
Target pH 7.5–8.5. Perth tap usually sits around 7.2–7.8, which is fine. The lake salt won't affect pH meaningfully. If you want to push it higher, add a tiny pinch (⅛ tsp) of baking soda.
4
Warm the water
Bring to 26–28 °C before adding eggs. A small aquarium heater or placing the hatchery near your tank works well.
🥚

Adding Eggs

Dosing for 1 litre
1
Measure eggs
Dose between ¼ tsp (~0.5g) and 1 tsp (~2–3g) depending on how much you want to produce. ¼ tsp gives a light batch with the best hatch rate; ½ tsp is a reliable standard batch; 1 tsp is the safe upper limit for 1L. Beyond 1 tsp, nauplii deplete oxygen faster than aeration can keep up and hatch rates drop.
2
Add eggs to water
Pour the measured eggs into the prepared saltwater. Do not add eggs to dry vessel first — wet them immediately to avoid clumping.
3
Confirm aeration is running
Eggs must be kept in constant suspension. Start your airline/airstone before or as you add the eggs. No aeration = poor hatch rate.
🌡️

Hatching Process

24–36 hours to nauplii
1
Aerate vigorously
Run a strong airstone to keep all eggs tumbling. The hatchery should look like a snow globe. Insufficient aeration is the #1 cause of failed hatches.
2
Maintain temperature
Keep water at 26–28 °C throughout. At this temperature, expect hatching in 24h. Cooler water (24 °C) takes 36–48h. Do not let it drop below 20 °C.
3
Provide light (optional but helpful)
A small light source near the hatchery for the first few hours encourages hatching. Artemia eggs are photosensitive at the start of incubation.
4
Observe for nauplii
After 24h, shine a torch into the hatchery. You should see tiny orange/amber dots swimming in a swirling cloud. Unhatched shells are typically pale and still.
Quick Reference
Salinity25–30 ppt
Temperature26–28 °C
Hatch time24–36 h
pH target7.5–8.5
🍤

Harvesting

Separating nauplii from shells
1
Stop aeration
Turn off the airstone and let the hatchery sit undisturbed for 10–15 minutes. Hatched nauplii swim to light; dead eggs and shells sink or float.
2
Concentrate the nauplii
Shine a torch or small light at the lower-middle of the vessel. Living nauplii will swarm toward the light, concentrating them for easy collection.
3
Siphon or pour off nauplii
Using a pipette, airline tube, or slow pour, collect the orange cloud of nauplii from the lit zone. Try to leave shells behind — they're indigestible and can foul water if fed in large quantities.
4
Rinse through a fine net
Pass the nauplii through a 100–150 µm brine shrimp net and rinse briefly with fresh RO or dechlorinated water. This removes excess salt before adding to your tank.
5
Feed immediately or freeze surplus
Nauplii are most nutritious within minutes of harvest. To freeze: spread rinsed nauplii in a thin layer on cling wrap or ice cube tray, freeze solid, then break into portions and store in a zip-lock bag. Frozen nauplii keep for several weeks.
